Why This Matters

Early commitment looks correct — until it isn't.

In noisy environments, committing too early produces answers that feel confident. The system says "bullish" and it looks right — until the signal shifts and it says "bearish" with the same confidence. No transition. No warning. Just a flip.

Most systems collapse uncertainty into a single answer.
When conditions shift, they flip.

This system preserves competing hypotheses and updates them over time — so it adapts without losing context.

The difference isn't accuracy on any single tick. It's behavior over time. A system that preserves alternatives can detect regime changes through gradual reweighting. A system that commits early can only detect them through reversal — which is always late, and always lossy.

Cross-Domain

This is not just a software problem.

In lithography, we see the same pattern: systems that collapse variability too early lose information and amplify error downstream. A process that commits to a single exposure parameter set — without preserving competing correction hypotheses — can't recover gracefully when conditions drift.

Preserving competing hypotheses is analogous to preserving signal under stochastic constraints. Whether the domain is financial regime detection or multi-head write optimization, the architecture is the same: maintain alternatives, update probabilistically, commit only when evidence converges.
